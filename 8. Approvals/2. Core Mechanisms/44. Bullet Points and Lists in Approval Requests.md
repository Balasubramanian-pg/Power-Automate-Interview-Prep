# 44. Bullet Points and Lists in Approval Requests

Canonical documentation for 44. Bullet Points and Lists in Approval Requests. This document defines the conceptual model, terminology, and standard usage patterns.

> [!NOTE]
> This documentation is implementation-agnostic and intended to serve as a stable reference.

## 1. Purpose and Problem Space
Describe why 44. Bullet Points and Lists in Approval Requests exists and the class of problems it addresses.
The purpose of using bullet points and lists in approval requests is to enhance the clarity, readability, and overall effectiveness of the request. This is particularly important in business, academic, and technical contexts where approval requests are common. The class of problems that bullet points and lists address includes disorganized or hard-to-follow requests, which can lead to misunderstandings, delays, or even the rejection of requests due to lack of clear information.

## 2. Conceptual Overview
Provide a high-level mental model of the topic.
The conceptual overview of bullet points and lists in approval requests involves understanding their role in structuring information in a clear and concise manner. This includes recognizing the types of information that are best presented in lists (e.g., multiple items, steps in a process) and how bullet points can be used to highlight key points or break down complex information into more digestible parts.

## 3. Terminology and Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Bullet Point | A typographical symbol used to introduce items in a list, making the list easier to read. |
| List | A series of items or tasks written or printed in a specific order. |
| Approval Request | A formal or informal ask for permission to proceed with a particular action, project, or decision. |
| Readability | The quality of being easy to read and understand, often enhanced through the use of bullet points and lists. |
| Clarity | The quality of being clear and easy to understand, which is crucial in approval requests to ensure prompt and informed decisions. |

## 4. Core Concepts
Explain the fundamental ideas that form the basis of this topic.
The core concepts of using bullet points and lists in approval requests include:
- **Organization**: Structuring the request in a logical and systematic way to facilitate understanding.
- **Clarity**: Ensuring that the request is easy to comprehend by avoiding ambiguity and using clear language.
- **Conciseness**: Presenting information in a brief and direct manner to hold the reader's attention and expedite the approval process.
- **Highlighting Key Points**: Using bullet points to draw attention to critical information that supports the approval request.

## 5. Standard Model
Describe the generally accepted or recommended model.
The standard model for incorporating bullet points and lists in approval requests typically involves:
1. **Introduction**: A brief overview of the request.
2. **Background Information**: Context that supports the request, which may include lists of relevant factors or bullet points highlighting key considerations.
3. **Specific Request**: Clearly stating what is being asked, potentially using bullet points to outline specific needs or requirements.
4. **Justification**: Providing reasons or benefits that support the approval of the request, often presented in a list format.
5. **Conclusion**: Summarizing the request and expressing appreciation for consideration, possibly including a call to action or next steps.

## 6. Common Patterns
Document recurring, accepted patterns.
Common patterns include:
- Using bullet points to list the benefits of approving a request.
- Incorporating numbered lists to outline steps or procedures related to the request.
- Employing bullet points to summarize key points or takeaways at the end of the request.

## 7. Anti-Patterns
Describe common but discouraged practices.
Anti-patterns to avoid include:
- Overusing bullet points or lists, which can make the request appear disjointed or difficult to follow.
- Failing to introduce or explain the purpose of lists or bullet points, leading to confusion.
- Using bullet points or lists in a way that disrupts the flow of the request, such as inserting them without clear transitions.
