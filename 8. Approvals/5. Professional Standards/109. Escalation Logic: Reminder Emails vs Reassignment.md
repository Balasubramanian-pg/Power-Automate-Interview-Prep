# 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment

Canonical documentation for 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment. This document defines the conceptual model, terminology, and standard usage patterns.

> [!NOTE]
> This documentation is implementation-agnostic and intended to serve as a stable reference.

## 1. Purpose and Problem Space
Describe why 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment exists and the class of problems it addresses.
The purpose of 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment is to provide a framework for managing and resolving pending tasks or issues in a timely and efficient manner. This logic addresses the class of problems related to task stagnation, missed deadlines, and inadequate communication. By implementing escalation logic, organizations can ensure that tasks are properly assigned, reminders are sent to relevant stakeholders, and reassignment occurs when necessary, thereby minimizing delays and improving overall productivity.

## 2. Conceptual Overview
Provide a high-level mental model of the topic.
The conceptual overview of 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment involves a multi-step process that includes task creation, assignment, reminder emails, and reassignment. The logic is triggered when a task is pending for an extended period, and a reminder email is sent to the assignee. If the task remains unresolved, the logic escalates the issue by reassigning it to a new stakeholder, such as a supervisor or team lead, to ensure timely resolution.

## 3. Terminology and Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Escalation Logic | A set of rules and processes that govern the escalation of pending tasks or issues to ensure timely resolution. |
| Reminder Email | An automated email sent to the assignee of a pending task to remind them of the task's existence and deadline. |
| Reassignment | The process of transferring a pending task from one stakeholder to another, typically to a supervisor or team lead, to ensure timely resolution. |
| Task Stagnation | A state where a task remains pending for an extended period without progress or resolution. |
| Stakeholder | An individual or group with an interest or involvement in the task or issue. |

## 4. Core Concepts
Explain the fundamental ideas that form the basis of this topic.
The core concepts of 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment include:
* Task management: The process of creating, assigning, and tracking tasks to ensure timely completion.
* Reminder emails: Automated emails sent to stakeholders to remind them of pending tasks and deadlines.
* Reassignment: The process of transferring tasks to new stakeholders to ensure timely resolution.
* Escalation thresholds: The criteria used to determine when a task should be escalated, such as time-based or event-based triggers.

## 5. Standard Model
Describe the generally accepted or recommended model.
The standard model for 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment involves the following steps:
1. Task creation and assignment
2. Reminder email sent to assignee after a predetermined period
3. Reassignment of task to a new stakeholder if the task remains unresolved after a second predetermined period
4. Escalation of the issue to a higher-level stakeholder, such as a manager or director, if the task remains unresolved after a third predetermined period.

## 6. Common Patterns
Document recurring, accepted patterns.
Common patterns in 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment include:
* Time-based escalation: Escalating tasks based on predetermined time intervals, such as daily or weekly reminders.
* Event-based escalation: Escalating tasks based on specific events, such as a task's deadline or a stakeholder's absence.
* Role-based escalation: Escalating tasks to stakeholders with specific roles or responsibilities, such as team leads or supervisors.

## 7. Anti-Patterns
Describe common but discouraged practices.
Anti-patterns in 109. Escalation Logic: Reminder Emails vs Reassignment include:
* Over-escalation: Escalating tasks too quickly or frequently, leading to unnecessary interruptions and distractions.
* Under-escalation: Failing to escalate tasks in a timely manner, leading to delays and stagnation.
* Inconsistent escalation: Applying inconsistent escalation logic, leading to confusion and misunderstandings among stakeholders.
